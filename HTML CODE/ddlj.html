The Science Behind Diwali Cleaning...

Traditionally, households are cleaned thoroughly before Diwali, symbolizing purification and readiness to welcome the goddess of wealth, Lakshmi. But this practice has a practical side: thorough cleaning before winter helps reduce dust, pests, and germs, which can thrive indoors in colder weather.


Mythological Links to Many Regions...

Diwali celebrates different legends in different regions. In Northern India, it’s associated with Lord Rama’s return to Ayodhya, while in Southern India, it commemorates Lord Krishna’s victory over the demon Narakasura. In Maharashtra and parts of Karnataka, it’s linked to the story of the demon king Bali, whose devotion earned him a place in the underworld.


Diwali's presence Beyond Hinduism...

Though Diwali is often seen as a Hindu festival, it’s also celebrated by Buddhist Newars in Nepal, some Bahá'ís, and Indian diaspora communities globally, including in countries like Trinidad and Tobago, Fiji, Guyana, Mauritius, Malaysia, and Singapore, where Diwali is a public holiday.


The Role of the Moon and Astrological Importance...

Diwali falls on Amavasya (the new moon night) in the Hindu month of Kartik. The darkest night of the year is symbolically chosen to emphasize the festival’s theme of bringing light into darkness. Many also consult astrologers to determine the auspicious times to perform pujas and begin new ventures on this day.
